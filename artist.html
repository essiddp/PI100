Rizal as an Artist

Other than writing, Jose Rizal also expresses himself artistically. He developed an interest in the arts at the young age of nine. “I had already such a liking for this art that our schoolmates called José Guevarra, another pupil, and myself the class painters,” as mentioned in his diary Rizal’s Own Story of His Life. As he grows older, he further enhances his skills and interest in the arts. As a visual artist, he created portraits and figurative representations of those he encountered during his exile in Dapitan and throughout his travels in Europe. An art exhibit in the National Museum of Fine Arts is dedicated to the works by and about Jose Rizal entitled “Inspiring the Nation, Dr. Jose Rizal: The National Hero in Art.”

Some of Jose Rizal’s famous artworks and background are listed below:

  • Saturnina Rizal in oil: This is currently displayed in the Rizal Shrine in Fort Santiago. Rizal painted this when he was 17 years old.
  • Dapitan church curtains in oil: Made in Dapitan in 1984. Fr Vicente Balaguer requested a painting of a backdrop for the church altar from Jose Rizal. A sketch was sent to Rizal in Barcelona, Spain. With a Filipina nun painter, Sister Agustina Montoya, and a native painter of Dapitan, Francisco Almirol, Rizal completed the backdrop painting. The painting was kept in the Ateneo de Manila Museum but was destroyed during World War II.
  • A pair of mother-of-pearl shells in oil: This was a token for Dona Leonor Valenzuela and was later passed to Dona Margarita Valenzuela.
  • Spanish coat of arms in watercolor: Painted during the San Rafael fiesta in Calamba in 1867.
  • Allegory on a pair of porcelain bases of the New Year celebration in oil: Painted in Berlin in 1886.
  • Christ crucified (Crayon painting): Created in 1875.
  • Immaculate Conception (Crayon painting): Made in Manila in 1874.
  • Portrait of Morayta (Crayon painting): Created in Barcelona in 1885.
