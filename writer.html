Rizal as a Writer

As most of us know, Jose Rizal used writing as his “sword” to fight for our country. He has written several literary works such as poems and even novels to address the issues that our country was facing back then. Some of his works that we have studied in our class are presented below:

A La Juventud Filipina

This literary piece, written in 1879, is a poem that aims to encourage the Filipino youth to take action for the betterment of the world and highlights the importance of a young person’s education and a good character. Rizal also made use of different references to other literary pieces to address this.

Read more: Memory NHCP or Studocu

El Amor Patrio

The first nationalist work of Rizal after he arrived at Europe. It is an essay that talks about the importance of understanding Philippine history and culture, as our country is beautiful in its own way and argues that love of country is a powerful force that inspires great deeds.

Read more: JSTOR

Noli Me Tangere

Published in 1887, Noli Me Tangere is considered Rizal's first novel. It's a social commentary that exposes the injustices and corruption within Philippine society during Spanish colonization. The story follows Crisóstomo Ibarra, a young man who returns to the Philippines after studying in Europe. He faces discrimination and abuse of power from the church and government officials, leading him to question the status quo. The title comes from the Latin phrase meaning "Touch me not," which foreshadows the tragic events and societal resistance the novel explores.

Read more: Rizal's Own Story of His Life

El Filibusterismo

Published in 1891, El Filibusterismo is the sequel to Noli Me Tangere. It takes a darker and more revolutionary approach. The protagonist, Simoun, is a mysterious jeweler who is actually Crisóstomo Ibarra, now a disillusioned and radicalized figure. He plots a violent uprising against the Spanish authorities. The novel delves into themes of revolution, societal decay, and the struggle for freedom.

Ang Liham sa mga Kababaihan ng Malolos

This 1889 essay is addressed to young women in Malolos who were advocating for opening a secondary school for girls. Despite being addressed to women, the essay will show you that the title is quite ironic from it's content.
